### **Simple explanation**
RBI just redid the rulebook for who can legally do foreign exchange business in India. Now, banks and other big Authorized Dealers (AD-I & II) can appoint small local “Forex Correspondents” to buy/sell foreign cash for travelers, under a principal-agent model. RBI also stopped giving new FFMC licences, set higher net-worth and turnover rules, and capped some transactions at ₹25 lakh.

### **The core idea**
1. **Expand reach, tighten control**: RBI is replacing old franchisee/money-changer models with Forex Correspondents tied to banks, while raising financial and compliance bars for ADs.
2. **One-stop FX access + risk checks**: More people can access forex services locally, but only strong, regulated entities can offer them.

### **Key concepts**
- **1. New agent model – Forex Correspondent (FxC)**: AD-I & II can appoint FxCs to buy/sell foreign currency notes, coins, travelers’ cheques, and act as MTSS sub-agents.
- **2. FFMC licences stopped**: No fresh Full Fledged Money Changer licences. Existing franchisee arrangements must end within 2 years from May 6, 2026.
- **3. AD category roles**: AD-I = banks, full current + capital account access. AD-II = NBFCs/money changers, limited trade up to ₹25 lakh per transaction. AD-III = innovative FX products.
- **4. Higher entry bars**: Min net worth ₹10 crore for AD-II, ₹2 crore for AD-III. Must be companies under Companies Act, 2013.
- **5. Turnover requirements**: Within 2 years of starting, AD-II must do ₹50 crore annual forex turnover, FFMCs ₹10 crore, and maintain it ongoing.
- **6. Only 3 categories now**: RBI will grant fresh authorisation only as AD-I, II, or III. FFMCs continue only if already licensed.
- **7. Regulations notified**: Foreign Exchange Management (Authorised Persons) Regulations, 2026, notified April 30, 2026, effective on Gazette publication.

### **One analogy**
Think of forex services like **medicine distribution**. Earlier, small chemist shops (FFMCs/franchisees) could sell directly. RBI now says: only big hospitals (AD banks) can stock meds, but they can appoint trained local clinics (Forex Correspondents) as agents. And chemist licences are closed — existing ones must upgrade or shut in 2 years.

### **Common confusions**
1. **“Anyone can become a Forex Correspondent” → No**
   Only companies meeting RBI criteria, appointed by an AD bank/NBFC. You can’t apply directly to RBI as FxC.

2. **“AD-II can do unlimited trade payments” → No**
   AD-II is capped at ₹25 lakh per foreign trade transaction. Full trade = AD-I banks only.

3. **“FFMCs are banned immediately” → No**
   No new FFMC licences, but existing ones can operate. Franchisees get 2 years till May 2028 to wind down.

# Slide 1 — RBI Revamps Foreign Exchange Authorisation Framework

### What Happened?

- Reserve Bank of India overhauled rules for entities dealing in foreign exchange
    
- AD Category-I and II entities can appoint Forex Correspondents under a principal-agent model
    
- Forex Correspondents can buy and sell foreign currency and travellers’ cheques
    
- AD Category-II entities face a ₹25 lakh cap per foreign trade transaction
    
- RBI introduced new net worth and forex turnover requirements for authorised entities
    

---

# Slide 2 — Why It Matters

### Why This Is Important

- Expands access to foreign exchange services across India
    
- Encourages innovation in forex-related financial products
    
- Strengthens regulation and financial stability in forex operations
    

### Key Numbers

- Transaction cap for AD-II: **₹25 lakh**
    
- Minimum net worth for AD-II: **₹10 crore**
    
- Minimum net worth for AD-III: **₹2 crore**
    
- Annual forex turnover requirement for AD-II: **₹50 crore**
    
- Annual forex turnover for FFMCs: **₹10 crore**
    

### Simple Definitions

- **Authorized Dealer (AD):** Entity permitted to handle foreign exchange
    
- **Forex Correspondent (FxC):** Agent conducting forex services
    
- **Forex Turnover:** Total foreign exchange business volume
    
- **Travellers’ Cheque:** Prepaid instrument used abroad
    
- **Principal-Agent Model:** Business conducted through authorised agents
